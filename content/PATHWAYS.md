# doswiki — The Master Pathways

**The connective tissue. Learn these ~16 pathways and every one of the 220 compounds becomes a lever you already understand.**

A researcher doesn't memorize compounds — they see the pathway and ask "which lever does this pull, and in which direction?" Each pathway below has: **what it is**, the **step-by-step cascade** (numbered, so you can visualize it), **plain English**, the **compounds that pull it** (cross-referenced to the compendium), and a **visualize-it** pointer. When you finish these, re-read any compound entry — it will read like a sentence in a language you now speak.

*How the levers work: a compound either turns a pathway UP (agonist/activator) or DOWN (antagonist/inhibitor). That's it. Every entry is one of those two moves on one of these pathways.*

---

## PATHWAY 1 — GPCR → cAMP → PKA (the universal relay)
**What it is:** G-protein-coupled receptors are the largest drug-target family. A ligand binds outside the cell; the receptor activates a G-protein inside; that changes the second messenger **cAMP**; cAMP activates **Protein Kinase A (PKA)**; PKA phosphorylates downstream targets.
**The cascade:**
1. Ligand binds the GPCR (7-pass membrane receptor).
2. G-protein activates (or inhibits) adenylate cyclase.
3. Adenylate cyclase changes cAMP level.
4. cAMP activates PKA.
5. PKA phosphorylates effectors (e.g., hormone-sensitive lipase → fat release; or ion channels).
**Plain English:** A doorbell (receptor) outside rings an internal alarm (cAMP) that mobilizes the house (PKA). A huge fraction of hormones and drugs are just different doorbells wired to this same alarm.
**Compounds that pull it:** Caffeine (blocks adenosine GPCR ↑cAMP), Clenbuterol/Ephedrine/Albuterol (β-adrenergic ↑), Yohimbine (blocks α-2, disinhibits), GLP-1 drugs (semaglutide/tirzepatide/retatrutide), PTH, glucagon.
**Visualize:** [Reactome GPCR signaling](https://reactome.org/) · receptor structures on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 2 — Nuclear hormone receptors → gene transcription (the slow, powerful lever)
**What it is:** Fat-soluble hormones pass through the membrane, bind a receptor *inside* the cell, and the pair travels to DNA to switch genes on/off. Slow (hours-days) but profound — it rewrites what proteins the cell makes.
**The cascade:**
1. Fat-soluble ligand crosses the membrane.
2. Binds its nuclear receptor in the cytoplasm/nucleus.
3. Receptor releases chaperone proteins, dimerizes.
4. Complex binds a **response element** (specific DNA sequence).
5. Transcription of target genes ↑ or ↓ → new proteins over hours-days.
**Plain English:** Instead of ringing a doorbell, these hormones walk into the control room and rewrite the building's blueprints. That's why steroids are slow to act but change your whole physiology.
**Compounds that pull it:** Testosterone/all AAS/SARMs (androgen receptor **AR**), Estradiol/SERMs (**ESR1/2**), Vitamin D (**VDR**), Thyroid T3/T4 (**THRB**), Cardarine (**PPARD**), Cortisol/ashwagandha-modulated (**NR3C1**), Retinoids (**RARA**), Ecdysterone (**ESR2**).
**Visualize:** Androgen receptor bound to DNA on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/) — you can see the DNA-binding "fingers."

## PATHWAY 3 — mTOR (the growth switch)
**What it is:** mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) senses nutrients and, when active, tells cells to grow, build protein, and divide. When inhibited, cells switch to repair and recycling (**autophagy**).
**The cascade:**
1. Amino acids (esp. leucine) + insulin/IGF signal abundance.
2. mTORC1 activates.
3. ↑ protein synthesis (muscle building), ↓ autophagy.
4. Chronic activation → growth but accumulated damage/aging.
**Plain English:** The "build" switch. Flip it on to grow muscle; flip it off periodically to clean house and age slower. The central tension of fitness-vs-longevity lives here — you can't maximize both at once.
**Compounds that pull it UP:** Leucine/whey/EAAs, HMB, insulin, IGF-1, creatine (indirectly). **DOWN:** Rapamycin (the definitive inhibitor), fasting, metformin (indirectly via AMPK).
**Visualize:** [Reactome mTOR signaling](https://reactome.org/).

## PATHWAY 4 — AMPK (the energy sensor)
**What it is:** AMPK is the low-fuel alarm. When cellular energy (ATP) drops, AMPK fires: burn fat, make mitochondria, stop storing, clean up. It is roughly the *opposite* of mTOR.
**The cascade:**
1. Energy demand ↑ → AMP:ATP ratio ↑.
2. AMPK activates.
3. ↑ fat oxidation, glucose uptake, mitochondrial biogenesis; ↓ fat/cholesterol synthesis; ↑ autophagy.
**Plain English:** The "we're running low, start burning and stop hoarding" switch. Exercise flips it naturally; several drugs mimic that.
**Compounds that pull it:** Metformin, Berberine, MOTS-c, alpha-lipoic acid, exercise. (Note: metformin's AMPK activation is also why it can blunt training gains — it partly duplicates the exercise signal.)
**Visualize:** [KEGG AMPK signaling pathway](https://www.genome.jp/kegg/).

## PATHWAY 5 — Nitric Oxide → cGMP (vasodilation)
**What it is:** Nitric oxide (NO) is a gas signal that relaxes blood-vessel walls, increasing blood flow. It raises **cGMP**, which relaxes smooth muscle. Phosphodiesterase-5 (PDE5) breaks cGMP down.
**The cascade:**
1. Substrate (arginine, or nitrate) → NO via eNOS (**NOS3**) or the nitrate pathway.
2. NO activates guanylate cyclase → ↑cGMP.
3. cGMP relaxes vascular smooth muscle → vasodilation → more flow, lower BP.
4. PDE5 degrades cGMP (so PDE5 inhibitors prolong the effect).
**Plain English:** The "open the pipes" system — better blood flow for pumps, endurance, blood pressure, and erections. You can push it from three angles: supply substrate (citrulline), supply NO directly (nitrate/beetroot), or stop the breakdown (PDE5 inhibitors).
**Compounds that pull it:** L-Citrulline, Beetroot/nitrate, Sildenafil/Tadalafil (PDE5 inhibitors), BPC-157 (VEGFR2→eNOS), agmatine.
**Visualize:** eNOS structure on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 6 — Receptor Tyrosine Kinases → PI3K/Akt (growth factors)
**What it is:** Growth-factor receptors that, when bound, phosphorylate themselves and ignite the **PI3K → Akt** cascade — driving growth, survival, glucose uptake, and angiogenesis. Feeds into mTOR.
**The cascade:**
1. Growth factor (insulin, IGF-1, VEGF, GH) binds its tyrosine-kinase receptor.
2. Receptor auto-phosphorylates.
3. PI3K → Akt activation.
4. ↑ glucose uptake (GLUT4), protein synthesis (→mTOR), cell survival, new blood vessels.
**Plain English:** The "grow and survive" wiring that growth factors use. It's how insulin stores nutrients, how IGF-1 builds tissue, and how BPC-157 grows new blood vessels to heal.
**Compounds that pull it:** Insulin (**INSR**), IGF-1 LR3 (**IGF1R**), GH/secretagogues (→IGF-1), BPC-157 (**VEGFR2/KDR**), MGF.
**Visualize:** [Reactome PI3K/Akt signaling](https://reactome.org/).

## PATHWAY 7 — GABA ⇄ Glutamate (the brain's brake and accelerator)
**What it is:** The nervous system's balance. **GABA** is the main *inhibitory* (calming) neurotransmitter; **glutamate** is the main *excitatory* (activating) one. Nearly all sedation, anxiety, and sleep pharmacology tunes this seesaw.
**The cascade:** GABA opens chloride channels (GABA-A) → neuron harder to fire → calm/sleep. Glutamate opens cation channels (NMDA/AMPA) → neuron fires → alertness/learning.
**Plain English:** Brake (GABA) vs accelerator (glutamate). Anxiety and insomnia are too much accelerator or too little brake; the fixes press one pedal or the other.
**Compounds that pull it:** *Toward calm (GABA↑):* L-theanine, magnesium, glycine, phenibut (GABA-B), apigenin, Z-drugs, alcohol. *Glutamate/learning:* racetams (AMPA modulators), ketamine (NMDA antagonist — paradoxically antidepressant).
**Visualize:** GABA-A receptor on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 8 — Monoamines: Dopamine / Norepinephrine / Serotonin
**What it is:** The three "mood and drive" neurotransmitters. Dopamine = motivation/reward/focus; norepinephrine = alertness/arousal; serotonin = mood/contentment/satiety. Drugs raise them by releasing more, blocking reuptake, or blocking breakdown (MAO).
**The cascade:** Precursor (tyrosine→dopamine→norepinephrine; tryptophan→serotonin) → released into synapse → acts on receptors → recycled by transporters (DAT/NET/SERT) or broken down by MAO.
**Plain English:** The chemistry of focus, drive, and mood. Stimulants flood the reward chemical; antidepressants keep serotonin around longer; each lever has a matching drug.
**Compounds that pull it:** Amphetamine/methylphenidate (block DAT/NET), Modafinil (weak DAT), Bupropion (DAT/NET), L-Tyrosine/Bromantane (precursor/synthesis), SSRIs (block SERT), 5-HTP/saffron (serotonin↑), Methylene blue (MAO inhibitor), nicotine (dopamine via nicotinic).
**Visualize:** Dopamine transporter on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/) · [PubChem dopamine](https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/compound/681).

## PATHWAY 9 — Cholinergic (acetylcholine: memory & muscle)
**What it is:** Acetylcholine drives memory formation, attention, and muscle contraction. You raise it by supplying choline (precursor) or by blocking its breakdown enzyme (acetylcholinesterase).
**The cascade:** Choline → acetylcholine (via ChAT enzyme) → acts on nicotinic (fast) or muscarinic (slow) receptors → broken down by acetylcholinesterase.
**Plain English:** The "memory and mind-muscle" chemical. Feed it (choline) or stop its cleanup (huperzine) to sharpen recall and focus.
**Compounds that pull it:** Alpha-GPC/Citicoline (precursors), Huperzine A (blocks breakdown), racetams (enhance signaling), nicotine (nicotinic agonist), Lion's Mane (indirectly via NGF).
**Visualize:** Nicotinic receptor on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 10 — HPTA (the testosterone control loop)
**What it is:** The hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis — a thermostat. The brain (hypothalamus→GnRH) tells the pituitary to release LH/FSH, which tell the testes to make testosterone; high testosterone/estrogen feeds back to shut the brain's signal off.
**The cascade:**
1. Hypothalamus releases GnRH.
2. Pituitary releases LH + FSH.
3. Testes make testosterone (LH) + sperm (FSH).
4. Testosterone/estradiol feed back → suppress GnRH (the off-switch).
**Plain English:** A self-regulating loop with a shut-off valve. This is *the* key to understanding steroids and PCT: adding outside testosterone trips the feedback valve, so your body stops making its own (and testes shrink). Recovery drugs work by re-opening the valve.
**Compounds that pull it:** Testosterone/AAS (suppress the loop), HCG (mimics LH, keeps testes on), Clomiphene/Enclomiphene/Tamoxifen (block estrogen feedback → restart the loop), Anastrozole (controls the estrogen that suppresses it).
**Visualize:** [Reactome — GnRH signaling](https://reactome.org/).

## PATHWAY 11 — HPA axis (the stress/cortisol loop)
**What it is:** The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis — your stress-response thermostat. Stress → CRH → ACTH → cortisol from the adrenals; cortisol feeds back to shut it down. Chronic activation raises cortisol, which suppresses testosterone, sleep, and recovery.
**The cascade:** Stress → hypothalamus CRH → pituitary ACTH → adrenal cortisol → (negative feedback).
**Plain English:** The stress dial. Turned up too long, it wrecks sleep, recovery, and testosterone. Adaptogens turn the dial back down.
**Compounds that pull it:** Ashwagandha (lowers cortisol), Rhodiola, Phosphatidylserine (blunts cortisol response), Tongkat Ali (lowers cortisol → frees testosterone). Cortisol itself binds the glucocorticoid receptor (Pathway 2).
**Visualize:** Cortisol structure [PubChem CID 5754](https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/compound/5754).

## PATHWAY 12 — Mitochondrial bioenergetics (the power plant)
**What it is:** Mitochondria make ATP via the electron transport chain (Complexes I–IV) using a proton gradient. This is where cellular energy — and a lot of aging — happens.
**The cascade:**
1. Nutrients → electrons fed into Complexes I–IV.
2. Electron flow pumps protons across the inner membrane.
3. The proton gradient drives ATP synthase → ATP.
4. CoQ10 shuttles electrons; damage/leak → reactive oxygen species (aging).
**Plain English:** Your cellular power stations. Keep them numerous, efficient, and undamaged for energy and longevity. Some compounds add spark plugs, some build more plants, some uncouple them to make heat (fat loss — or death, in DNP's case).
**Compounds that pull it:** CoQ10/Ubiquinol (electron carrier), PQQ (builds new mitochondria), Urolithin A (recycles damaged ones — mitophagy), Methylene blue (backup electron carrier), SS-31 (repairs membranes), DNP (uncoupler — heat, lethal), MOTS-c.
**Visualize:** [KEGG oxidative phosphorylation](https://www.genome.jp/kegg/).

## PATHWAY 13 — NAD⁺ / Sirtuins (the longevity coenzyme)
**What it is:** NAD⁺ is a coenzyme for energy metabolism and for **sirtuins** — enzymes that regulate aging genes, DNA repair, and mitochondrial health. NAD⁺ falls ~50% from age 20→50.
**The cascade:** Precursors (NMN/NR/niacin) → NAD⁺ → fuels sirtuins (SIRT1) + PARP DNA-repair → improved mitochondrial/metabolic function.
**Plain English:** A currency your longevity-and-repair enzymes spend, and it runs low with age. Refill the currency (NMN/NR) and the repair crews can work.
**Compounds that pull it:** NMN, NR, Niacin (raise NAD⁺); Resveratrol/Pterostilbene (activate sirtuins); metformin depletes it (offset with precursors).
**Visualize:** [Reactome NAD metabolism](https://reactome.org/) · SIRT1 on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 14 — NF-κB / Inflammation (the master inflammatory switch)
**What it is:** NF-κB is the transcription factor that turns on inflammatory genes (cytokines like IL-6, TNF-α). Chronic low-grade activation ("inflammaging") drives most age-related disease.
**The cascade:** Trigger (infection, damage, stress) → NF-κB moves to the nucleus → transcribes inflammatory cytokines → inflammation.
**Plain English:** The body's inflammation master switch. A little is protective (healing); stuck-on is corrosive (aging, pain, disease). Most "anti-inflammatory" supplements quiet this switch.
**Compounds that pull it DOWN:** Curcumin, Omega-3 (resolvins), Boswellia (via 5-LOX), Sulforaphane (via NRF2), ashwagandha; senolytics remove the inflammatory zombie cells that keep it lit.
**Visualize:** [Reactome NF-κB signaling](https://reactome.org/).

## PATHWAY 15 — Melanocortin (tanning, libido, appetite)
**What it is:** The melanocortin receptor family (MC1R–MC5R) governs skin pigment (MC1R), appetite and libido (MC3R/MC4R). Activated by α-MSH.
**The cascade:** α-MSH (or a drug) binds MC1R (→melanin/tanning) or MC3R/MC4R (→↓appetite, ↑libido).
**Plain English:** One receptor family, several jobs — tan the skin, curb hunger, spark desire — depending on which subtype you hit.
**Compounds that pull it:** PT-141/Bremelanotide (MC3R/MC4R → libido), Setmelanotide (MC4R → appetite, approved), Melanotan II (MC1R → tanning + MC4R).
**Visualize:** MC4R structure on [Mol\*/PDB](https://www.rcsb.org/).

## PATHWAY 16 — Cellular Senescence & Autophagy (the clean-up crews)
**What it is:** Two aging-critical housekeeping systems. **Senescence:** cells that stop dividing but won't die, leaking inflammation ("zombie cells"); **senolytics** clear them. **Autophagy:** the cell recycling damaged parts; it declines with age and is a longevity lever.
**The cascade:** Damage → some cells become senescent (secrete inflammatory SASP). Separately, low mTOR / high AMPK / fasting → autophagy genes (ATG) → damaged components recycled.
**Plain English:** Aging is partly a garbage problem: zombie cells that need removing (senolytics) and cellular junk that needs recycling (autophagy). Several longevity compounds do one or the other.
**Compounds that pull it:** *Senolytics (kill zombies):* Fisetin, Quercetin, Dasatinib+Quercetin. *Autophagy (recycle junk):* Spermidine, rapamycin (via mTOR↓), fasting, metformin, urolithin A (mitochondria-specific).
**Visualize:** [Reactome autophagy](https://reactome.org/).

## The whole map in one paragraph
Everything in the wiki is one of two moves — **up or down** — on one of these sixteen pathways. Muscle-building lives on **mTOR + androgen-receptor + PI3K/Akt**. Fat loss lives on **GPCR/cAMP (thermogenics), GLP-1 GPCRs, and AMPK**. Focus/mood lives on **monoamines, cholinergic, and GABA/glutamate**. Longevity lives on **mTOR (down), AMPK (up), NAD⁺, senescence/autophagy, and mitochondria**. Hormones run through **HPTA, HPA, and nuclear receptors**. Recovery and blood flow run through **NO/cGMP, PI3K/Akt, and NF-κB (down)**. Learn the sixteen levers, and 220 compounds collapse into a handful of ideas you can reason about like an expert — which is the entire point of this wiki.
